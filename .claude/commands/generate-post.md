## Step 1 — Read the data

Read `pipeline-output.json`. Extract:
- Total number of deals analyzed
- Count of HEALTHY / WATCH / AT RISK deals
- The most interesting AT RISK deal (the one with the sharpest or most specific risk factors — not just the most flags, but the most revealing one)
- One HEALTHY deal that shows the system working correctly — strong next step, realistic forecast note
- Any pattern worth calling out (e.g., multiple deals flagged for vague next steps, or a cluster of deals with no activity)

## Step 3 — Draft the post

Write a LinkedIn post using these rules:

### Voice
- First person. Kal built this himself — he is not demoing software, he is reflecting on what he learned.
- Conversational but confident. No hype language, no jargon, no em dashes.
- Sounds like someone talking to peers in sales leadership, not explaining a tool to engineers.
- Short sentences. Active voice. Never use the phrase "game changer," "leverage," "unlock," "AI-powered," or "I built a tool."

### Structure (follow this order, no headers or labels in the post itself)

**Opening hook — the problem (2–3 sentences)**
Start with the situation every sales manager knows: pipeline reviews that feel like theater. Deals that look fine until they aren't. The gap between what's in the CRM and what's actually happening. Do not start with "I." Do not open with a question.

**The before (2–3 sentences)**
What the old way looked like: manual review, gut feel, AEs giving optimistic updates with no accountability. Specific details over generalities. One concrete example of what gets missed.

**What he built and what it does (3–4 sentences)**
One sentence on what it is (an agent that reads a CRM export and runs each deal through a structured framework). The rest on what it produces: specific call-outs, named risk factors, exact next steps with a person and a date attached. Reference MEDPIC by name once — frame it as the methodology, not the acronym. Do not explain MEDPIC. Do not say "AI."

**A concrete before/after from this run (2–4 sentences)**
Pull a real example from the data. A deal that had been sitting for 26 days with a next step that just said "check in." Or a $320k deal in Discovery with no next step at all and no activity in 23 days. Use the actual deal details — real numbers, real stage names. Show what the system flagged and what it recommended instead. This is the most important paragraph.

**What equivalent SaaS costs (1–2 sentences)**
Name a real competitor in the revenue intelligence / pipeline inspection space (Gong, Clari, Chorus, People.ai). Give a realistic annual cost for a small sales team (5–10 reps). Frame it as: he built the core functionality himself, the output reads the same way, and it cost him a few hours and API usage fees.

**The closing insight — what surprised him (2–3 sentences)**
Use the answer from Step 2. This should feel like a genuine reflection, not a lesson learned. It can be uncomfortable. It should make a sales manager nod.

**Call to action (1 sentence, optional)**
Only include if it feels natural. Something like "Happy to share the prompts if you want to run it on your own pipeline." Not a pitch. Not a form. One line.

---

### Format rules
- No bullet points anywhere in the post
- No headers or bold text
- Paragraph breaks between each section (blank line)
- Total length: 250–350 words
- End without a sign-off or hashtags unless asked
